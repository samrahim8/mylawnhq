The One Thing

Sharpen your mower blades before the season starts.

This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do this week. Dull blades don't cut grass ‚Äî they tear it. And torn grass tips turn brown within 24 hours, making your lawn look hazy and stressed even when it's perfectly healthy.

Worse: torn tissue is an open invitation for fungal disease. Dollar spot, brown patch, and leaf spot all enter through damaged blade tips. You can prevent a lot of summer headaches with 20 minutes and a $10 file right now.

The test: Pull a handful of grass and look at the tips. Clean, diagonal cut? You're good. Frayed, white, shredded edges? Time to sharpen. Most blades need sharpening every 20-25 hours of mowing ‚Äî for most homeowners, that's 2-3 times per season.

Ask Larry

"Should I bag my clippings or mulch them back into the lawn?"

‚Äî Dave, Orlando

Mulch them. Every time.

Grass clippings are free fertilizer. They break down quickly and return up to 25% of your lawn's annual nitrogen needs back to the soil. That's real money you're throwing away when you bag.

The only time to bag: if you're cutting off more than one-third of the blade height in a single mow. Those heavy clumps can smother the grass underneath and create dead spots. But here's the thing ‚Äî if you're removing that much, you waited too long between mows. The real fix is mowing more frequently, not bagging.

Pro tip: If clippings are visible after mowing, do a second pass perpendicular to your first. It disperses them better than any bagger ever could.
